## Today's Digest

### Day 2: Historical Roots of the US-China Rivalry

To understand the current landscape of the US-China rivalry, one must delve into its intricate historical roots — a narrative interwoven with ambition, paranoia, and pivotal decisions that shaped the world as we know it today. This rivalry did not emerge in a vacuum; it is a product of centuries of interaction, ideological conflict, and strategic miscalculations that have left an indelible mark on international relations.

#### The Early Encounters: A Clash of Civilizations

The seeds of the US-China rivalry can arguably be traced back to the mid-19th century, during a period marked by imperial expansion and colonial ambition. The Opium Wars (1839-1842 and 1856-1860) are critical in this narrative; they exemplify the violent clash of civilizations. The British, in their quest for profit, exported opium to China, leading to widespread addiction and societal upheaval. When the Qing Dynasty attempted to curtail this trade, the British responded with military force, resulting in humiliating defeats for China and the Treaty of Nanking, which forced China to cede Hong Kong and open several ports to foreign trade. This marked the beginning of what the Chinese refer to as the "Century of Humiliation," a term that still resonates deeply in Chinese national consciousness.

On the other side of the Pacific, the young United States was grappling with its own identity. The American Civil War (1861-1865) and the subsequent Reconstruction era diverted attention from foreign policy. However, in the late 19th century, the US began to assert itself on the global stage, driven by a combination of Manifest Destiny and a burgeoning sense of nationalistic pride. The US's interest in China was further piqued by the Open Door Policy (1899), which aimed to ensure equal trading rights for all foreign nations in China. This policy reflected the growing American desire to project influence in Asia, setting the stage for future engagements.

#### Key Figures: The Overlooked Strategist

Amid the tumultuous history of US-China relations, one figure often overlooked is John Hay, US Secretary of State from 1898 to 1905. Hay was instrumental in crafting the Open Door Policy and navigating the complexities of international diplomacy in a rapidly changing world. His vision of a multilateral approach to trade in China was revolutionary for its time, emphasizing cooperation over imperialistic competition. While his name does not resonate with the same weight as contemporaries like Theodore Roosevelt or Woodrow Wilson, Hay's diplomatic efforts laid foundational stones for what would become a long-standing American engagement with Asia.

Moreover, Hay's vision was not merely about economic gain; it was infused with a belief in American exceptionalism and the notion that the US had a moral obligation to promote democracy and freedom. This ideological underpinning would later clash with China’s own narrative of sovereignty and resurgence, igniting tensions that would evolve into the current rivalry.

#### The Cold War and the Seeds of Modern Rivalry

The trajectory of US-China relations took a significant turn during the Cold War. Initially, the US viewed China through the lens of anti-communism, particularly after the Chinese Communist Party (CCP) seized power in 1949 under Mao Zedong. The Korean War (1950-1953) further solidified this perception, as China intervened on behalf of North Korea, pitting it directly against American forces. The ideological battle lines were drawn, with the US embracing a strategy of containment, which prioritized limiting the spread of communism globally.

However, the 1970s ushered in a dramatic pivot with President Richard Nixon’s historic visit to China in 1972. This diplomatic breakthrough was driven by a combination of geopolitical calculation and a shared interest in counterbalancing the Soviet Union. The ensuing rapprochement opened doors for economic engagement and cultural exchange, fostering a relationship that seemed, at least on the surface, to benefit both nations.

#### The Accidental Rivalry: Miscalculations and Misunderstandings

Yet, the optimism of the 1970s would not last. As China embarked on its economic reforms under Deng Xiaoping in the late 1970s, the US's initial support morphed into a complicated dance of dependency and distrust. The Tiananmen Square massacre in 1989 was a watershed moment that shattered any lingering illusions of a harmonious partnership. The US response — sanctions and a diplomatic freeze — underscored the precarious nature of their relations, sowing seeds of resentment in China regarding American moral superiority.

The turn of the millennium saw China rise as an economic powerhouse, leading to a perception in the US of China as a strategic competitor rather than a partner. This shift was exacerbated by the 2008 financial crisis, which, contrary to expectations, did not derail China's growth but rather solidified its position as a formidable global player.

### Looking Ahead: The Mechanics of Competition

As we turn our gaze to the present, understanding the historical nuances of US-China relations is essential for deciphering the mechanics of their current rivalry. The historical grievances, ideological divides, and strategic missteps have all contributed to a complex tapestry of competition that is as much about power as it is about identity. 

In Day 3, we will explore the mechanics of this rivalry, examining how economic interdependence, military posturing, and technological competition are reshaping the global order and the implications for future relations between these two great powers. The journey through history not only reveals the roots of conflict but also frames the contours of a future that remains uncertain and fraught with challenges.

## Reference Pick

**The Rise and Fall of the Great Powers — Paul Kennedy**

Paul Kennedy’s seminal work is a cornerstone for understanding the historical roots of great power competition. He meticulously examines the interplay between economic strength and military capability from the Renaissance to the present, providing a sweeping narrative that contextualizes how past great powers navigated their rise and decline. This book not only highlights key figures and pivotal moments but also introduces the concept of "imperial overstretch," a crucial lens through which to view contemporary rivalries, particularly between the U.S. and China. Kennedy's analytical depth, combined with his engaging writing style, makes this a must-read for anyone seeking to grasp the intricate dynamics that have shaped global power structures over centuries.
