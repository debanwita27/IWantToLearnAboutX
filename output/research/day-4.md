## Today's Digest

### Day 4: Controversies and Unsolved Problems in the US-China Rivalry

In the intricate web of US-China relations, where every diplomatic exchange, trade negotiation, and military maneuver is scrutinized, controversies abound. The rivalry between these two global giants is not merely a clash of economic interests or military might; it represents a deeper ideological struggle that challenges the very frameworks through which we understand international relations. This day’s exploration will delve into some of the most pressing controversies, unsolved problems, and edge cases that characterize the ongoing great power competition.

#### Open Question: The Nature of the Rivalry

One of the most significant open questions within the field is whether the US-China rivalry constitutes a new Cold War or a fundamentally different phenomenon. Scholars and policymakers diverge sharply on this issue. Proponents of the "new Cold War" narrative, such as Graham Allison, argue that the systemic competition bears striking similarities to the US-Soviet confrontations of the mid-20th century. They point to the ideological underpinnings—capitalism versus authoritarianism—and the potential for proxy conflicts across the globe, much like during the Cold War.

Conversely, others, like Kishore Mahbubani, contend that framing the competition in Cold War terms is misleading. They argue that the globalized economy and interconnected world of today create a context where direct military confrontation is less likely and economic interdependence is more pronounced. This perspective emphasizes the potential for collaboration on global challenges like climate change and pandemics, arguing that the rivalry is more complex and multifaceted than a binary ideological struggle.

The debate raises critical questions: Can the US and China coexist peacefully, or are we destined for inevitable conflict? Is the pursuit of a "Thucydides Trap"—the idea that a rising power inevitably causes fear in an established power, leading to war—inevitable, or can it be avoided through strategic engagement? These questions remain hotly contested, with implications for global stability.

#### Contested Knowledge: The Economic Interdependence

Another widely held belief that deserves scrutiny is the notion that economic interdependence between the US and China serves as a stabilizing force in their relationship. Many experts assert that the deep trade ties and investment flows create a mutual interest in avoiding conflict. The idea is that both nations are economically tethered; thus, a war would be too costly for either side.

However, this view is increasingly challenged. The reality is that while trade between the US and China reached record highs during certain periods—totaling over $700 billion in 2022—the benefits of this interdependence are unevenly distributed and fraught with tension. Critics point to the rising protectionist sentiments in the US and the increasing push by China for technological self-sufficiency as evidence that economic ties can just as easily be weaponized. The imposition of tariffs, export controls, and sanctions has highlighted how fragile this interdependence can be. 

Moreover, the COVID-19 pandemic exposed vulnerabilities in global supply chains, prompting many companies to reconsider their reliance on China. This has led to discussions about "decoupling," where nations seek to disentangle their economies from Chinese influence, a move that some argue could lead to increased tensions rather than the stability that interdependence is presumed to foster.

#### Edge Cases: The Role of Regional Allies

An additional area of contention lies in the role of regional allies in the US-China rivalry. The United States has historically relied on alliances in the Asia-Pacific, such as those with Japan, South Korea, and Australia, to counterbalance China's influence. The Quad (Quadrilateral Security Dialogue) and AUKUS (the trilateral security pact between Australia, the UK, and the US) are examples of such strategic partnerships designed to enhance collective security in the region.

However, the effectiveness and commitment of these allies can be unpredictable. For instance, Japan’s position has been complicated by its own economic ties to China, and South Korea's response to the US-China rivalry has been shaped by its security concerns regarding North Korea. Furthermore, the role of ASEAN (Association of Southeast Asian Nations) member states—many of which are economically intertwined with China—adds another layer of complexity. Some ASEAN countries are wary of being drawn into a US-China conflict, preferring to maintain a neutral stance that allows them to benefit from both powers.

In this context, an important edge case arises: can the US effectively rally its allies around a cohesive strategy against China, or will differing national interests lead to fragmentation? The answer to this question could significantly influence the trajectory of the US-China rivalry.

#### Conclusion

The US-China rivalry is marked by a rich tapestry of controversies, unsolved problems, and contested beliefs. As we continue to unravel the complexities of great power competition, it becomes clear that simplistic narratives do not capture the depth of this multifaceted relationship. The rivalry is not just about who will dominate the international order; it encompasses profound questions about coexistence, economic ties, and the role of allies. As experts debate these issues, the outcome will shape not only the fate of bilateral relations but also the future of global governance.
